üåä Introduction to the Urinary System:

The urinary system, also known as the excretory system, plays a vital role in maintaining the body‚Äôs internal balance. It removes waste products, especially nitrogenous wastes like urea, from the blood and helps regulate water, salts, and pH levels. This system ensures that the body stays free from toxic substances and maintains a healthy fluid balance. It consists of a set of organs that filter blood, produce urine, and eliminate it from the body. Without this system, waste would accumulate to dangerous levels, affecting nearly every other organ system.

üî∏üö∞ Filtration and Urine Formation (Kidneys):

The filtration and urine formation phase takes place in the kidneys, which are paired, bean-shaped organs located in the lower back on either side of the spine. Their primary role is to filter blood and form urine through millions of functional units called nephrons. Each nephron consists of a glomerulus, a tiny bundle of capillaries, and a Bowman‚Äôs capsule that collects the filtered fluid. This fluid then travels through renal tubules, where essential nutrients, ions, and water are reabsorbed, while waste products are left behind to form urine. The kidneys also maintain electrolyte balance, regulate blood pressure via the renin-angiotensin system, and stimulate red blood cell production through the hormone erythropoietin. The key components here include: kidneys, nephrons, glomeruli, Bowman‚Äôs capsule, renal tubules, renal arteries and veins, renin, and erythropoietin.

üî∏üö¶ Urine Transport System

Once urine is formed, the transport system takes over. Ureters, slender muscular tubes, carry urine from each kidney to the urinary bladder using wave-like contractions known as peristalsis. The bladder is a stretchable, hollow organ that temporarily stores urine until it's ready to be released. Its walls are lined with detrusor muscle, which contracts during urination to push urine out. The process is regulated by sphincter muscles‚Äîthe internal sphincter operates involuntarily, while the external sphincter offers voluntary control. Together, these parts allow precise management of urine flow and prevent leakage. Key components of this section include: ureters, urinary bladder, detrusor muscle, internal sphincter, and external sphincter.
