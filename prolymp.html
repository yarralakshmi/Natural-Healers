The lymph fluid and circulatory network make up the transport system of the lymphatic system. Lymph is a clear fluid that originates from tissue spaces and carries white blood cells, especially lymphocytes, along with nutrients, waste, and excess fluid. This fluid is collected by tiny vessels called lymphatic capillaries and travels through larger lymphatic vessels, passing through lymph nodes for filtration. The movement of lymph is aided by valves, muscle contractions, and body movements, since the system lacks a central pump like the heart. Eventually, the lymph is returned to the bloodstream through major vessels called lymphatic ducts‚Äîthe thoracic duct and the right lymphatic duct. This circulation helps maintain fluid balance, remove waste, and support the immune system by continuously monitoring and transporting immune cells throughout the body.

üî∏üß† Lymph Nodes & Immune Surveillance

Lymph nodes are small, bean-shaped structures scattered throughout the body. They act as checkpoints that filter lymph fluid and help detect and destroy harmful invaders like bacteria, viruses, and cancer cells.

As lymph flows through the nodes, white blood cells such as lymphocytes and macrophages identify and attack pathogens. This process is known as immune surveillance, which helps protect the body from infections. Lymph nodes are commonly found in areas such as the neck, armpits, groin, chest, and abdomen. When the body is fighting an infection, nearby lymph nodes may swell as they become active.

Thus, lymph nodes play a vital role in both filtering harmful substances and triggering immune responses to keep the body healthy.‚ú®
