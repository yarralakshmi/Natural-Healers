Betel Leaf (Piper betle), a glossy, heart-shaped evergreen vine, has been cultivated for thousands of years across South and Southeast Asia. Its history can be traced back over 5,000 years, with references in ancient Sanskrit texts, including the Charaka Samhita and Sushruta Samhita, where it was praised for its medicinal and digestive properties. Indigenous to regions like India, Sri Lanka, and Malaysia, betel leaf was grown in shaded courtyards, temple gardens, and forested groves. Its widespread cultivation was supported by the tropical climate, making it a household plant in traditional communities

The betel leaf holds a sacred place in Indian and Southeast Asian culture. In Hindu rituals, it is offered to deities as a symbol of prosperity, purity, and respect. During weddings and festivals, betel leaves are exchanged as auspicious tokens, symbolizing love, fertility, and good fortune. In Southeast Asia, especially in Myanmar, Thailand, and Cambodia, betel chewing with areca nut and lime was an integral social custom, believed to refresh breath, aid digestion, and strengthen social bonds. Betel leaf was not just a herb but a cultural tradition woven into daily and spiritual life.
